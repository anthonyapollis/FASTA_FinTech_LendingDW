Section 01

The Business Problem

Why does a FinTech lender need a machine learning data warehouse? What breaks when you don't have one?

The Lending Blindspot

FASTA is a South African FinTech lender offering short-term personal loans. Like most lenders, they face a fundamental tension: approve too many loans and bad debt climbs; approve too few and revenue suffers. Without a data-driven scoring system, underwriters rely on manual credit bureau checks and gut feel — slow, inconsistent, and expensive at scale.

Four specific pain points motivated this platform:

  • Credit defaults costing margin: A 23.5% default rate on the loan book — every percentage point costs hundreds of thousands of rands in write-offs.
  • Affordability guesswork: Loan officers estimate disposable income manually, leading to over-lending to borrowers who can't repay and under-lending to creditworthy customers.
  • No early warning on churn: Customers who stop repaying give off early signals (payment timing, contact avoidance) that a human can't process at scale, but a model can.
  • Wasted marketing budget: R500,000 per month spent across 7 acquisition channels with no data on which channels bring in the best borrowers — lowest default rates, highest repayment quality.
The Core Question
For every loan application: "Will this person repay — and if so, how much can they actually afford?" For every existing customer: "Are they about to stop paying, and which channel brought them to us?"

Why a Data Warehouse + ML Pipeline?

A standalone model in a spreadsheet doesn't scale. FASTA needed an end-to-end automated system that:

  • Ingests raw loan application and transaction data daily
  • Cleans, transforms, and enriches it through standardised layers
  • Trains and scores four ML models every Saturday morning
  • Automatically retrains models when performance degrades
  • Refreshes Power BI dashboards so analysts see live risk scores

That is exactly what this platform delivers — and why Microsoft Fabric was chosen as the technology backbone.

Why Microsoft Fabric?
Fabric unifies data engineering (Data Factory pipelines), storage (OneLake Lakehouse with Delta tables), ML experimentation (MLflow integration), and business intelligence (Power BI semantic models) in a single governed platform — eliminating the fragmented toolchain of separate warehouses, orchestrators, and BI tools.

The Affordability Problem

Responsible lending regulation (National Credit Act, South Africa) requires lenders to assess affordability before granting credit. Failing this assessment leads to over-indebtedness — the borrower cannot service the loan, defaults, and the lender writes off the debt. The model must predict how much is left over after all existing obligations are met.

The formula: Affordability = Gross Income − Tax − Living Expenses − Existing Debt Repayments. The model learns the interaction terms between these variables, including non-linear relationships captured by polynomial feature expansion (degree 2).

Why Polynomial ElasticNet?
A standard linear model (R²=0.912) misses the interaction between income and existing debt — a high-income borrower with many obligations may be less affordable than a moderate-income borrower with few. Adding polynomial degree-2 features captures these interactions. ElasticNet's L1+L2 regularisation then prunes the irrelevant polynomial terms, preventing overfitting.

Churn Model — Who Is About to Stop Paying?

Churn in a lending context is defined as: a customer who was active but misses 2+ consecutive payments and has no approved arrangement in place. Identifying churners 30 days in advance allows collections teams to intervene proactively — a phone call costs far less than a default write-off.

A perfect AUC in production would be suspicious — this is a synthetic dataset with a 45-day churn label generated from a simple rule. On real collections data with genuinely ambiguous borderline cases, AUC will realistically land at 0.80–0.90. The model architecture, feature engineering pipeline, and MLflow registration are all production-ready.

Promise-to-Pay (PTP) Model — Will They Follow Through?

When a collections agent contacts a customer in arrears, the customer often makes a promise: "I'll pay by Friday." Only 1 in 3 (34.4%) actually keep that promise. The PTP model predicts which promises are genuine, allowing agents to prioritise follow-up calls and escalate to legal action earlier for low-PTP customers.

Improvement Opportunity
A PTP AUC of 0.56 is modest — borderline better than random. Feature engineering on contact attempt history, time-of-day preference, previous promise track record, and payment channel usage is expected to push this to 0.70+. This is flagged in the model health log for the next retrain cycle.

How These Models Work Together

In production, these two models feed a collections prioritisation matrix: customers are scored on both axes (churn risk × PTP probability) and segmented into four quadrants that determine the collections strategy — from automated SMS for low-risk/high-PTP to immediate agent escalation for high-risk/low-PTP.

The Channel Quality Problem

Marketing teams measure success in cost-per-lead or conversion rate. But for a lender, a "conversion" that defaults in 60 days is a loss, not a win. The channel ROI scorecard reframes the question: which channels bring borrowers who repay?

The quality score is a composite metric weighted across three dimensions:

  • Default rate (40% weight) — Channels with low default rates score higher. Email borrowers default at only 12.3%.
  • Repayment collection rate (35% weight) — How much of the outstanding balance is actually collected.
  • Average credit score (25% weight) — A proxy for underlying borrower quality.

Automatic Retraining — When Does the Model Update Itself?

Every Saturday after scoring, the pipeline checks one condition: credit_risk_AUC < retrain_threshold_auc (default 0.70). If true:

Step 1 — Immediate
Teams Alert Fires
Webhook posts a card to the data team channel: model name, current AUC, threshold, timestamp.
Step 2 — Within Minutes
Full Retrain Notebook Launches
nb_fabric_ml_retrain_full retrains all 4 models on the last 90 days of data using a larger Spark session (56GB, 8 cores, 4 executors). Estimated runtime: ~90 minutes.
Step 3 — After Retrain
New Model Versions Registered in MLflow
Each model gets a new version in fasta.models.*. The registry keeps the previous version for rollback.
Step 4 — Audit
RETRAINED Row Written to gold._model_health_log
Before/after AUC, retrain timestamp, training window size, and operator (SYSTEM) all recorded for compliance.
